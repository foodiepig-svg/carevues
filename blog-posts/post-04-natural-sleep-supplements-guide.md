## What Makes a Sleep Supplement Actually Work?

Before we get into specific products, let's set some ground rules.

A good sleep supplement, natural or otherwise, should:

- **Help you fall asleep faster** (reduce sleep onset latency)
- **Improve sleep quality** (more deep sleep, fewer awakenings)
- **Be safe for regular use** (not habit-forming)
- **Have evidence behind the active ingredients**

The "natural" label means very little on its own. Arsenic is natural. So is cyanide. What matters is the specific compound, the dose, and the evidence for it.

## The Big Four: Natural Sleep Supplements Ranked

### 1. Magnesium — Best Overall

**What it is:** A mineral involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body, including neurotransmitter regulation and muscle relaxation.

**The evidence:** Multiple studies show magnesium can improve sleep quality, particularly in people who are deficient. A 2021 randomised controlled trial found magnesium supplementation significantly improved insomnia severity scores, sleep efficiency, and serum melatonin levels.

**How it works:** Magnesium helps activate the parasympathetic nervous system (your "rest and digest" mode). It also regulates GABA, the neurotransmitter that tells your brain to slow down.

**Best form for sleep:** Magnesium glycinate or magnesium citrate. Glycinate is gentler on the stomach; citrate has better absorption.

**Dose:** 200–400mg taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Start low.

### 2. Melatonin — Best for Shift Workers and Jet Lag

**What it is:** A hormone produced by the pineal gland that regulates your circadian rhythm — your body's internal sleep-wake clock.

**The evidence:** Melatonin is one of the most well-studied sleep supplements. It's effective for:
- **Jet lag** (strongly supported by evidence)
- **Shift work disorder** (moderate evidence)
- **Sleep onset insomnia** in older adults (moderate evidence)
- General insomnia in children (paediatric use is well-established)

For otherwise healthy adults with situational sleep problems, the evidence is moderate but meaningful.

**How it works:** Melatonin doesn't force you to sleep — it signals to your brain that it's nighttime. Think of it as resetting your body clock rather than sedating you.

**Dose:** 0.5–5mg, taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Lower doses (0.5–1mg) are often as effective as higher ones, and less likely to cause morning grogginess.

**Important:** Melatonin is a prescription medicine in Australia for children under 16, and over-the-counter for adults 16+. It is **not** suitable for everyone — talk to your GP if you have any hormonal conditions.

### 3. Valerian Root — Mild but Real Effect

**What it is:** An herbal remedy derived from the root of the Valeriana officinalis plant, used since Roman times as a calming agent.

**The evidence:** Here's where it gets interesting. Early reviews dismissed valerian as placebo. More recent meta-analyses — including a 2020 review in the *Journal of Ethnopharmacology* — found that valerian significantly improved sleep quality and reduced time to fall asleep compared to placebo. The effect is mild to moderate, not dramatic.

**How it works:** Valerian appears to increase GABA levels in the brain, similar to how some anti-anxiety medications work (though much more gently).

**Dose:** 300–600mg of standardized extract (containing 0.8% valerenic acid) taken 30–60 minutes before bed.

**Taste warning:** Valerian tea tastes like dirt. The capsules are significantly more palatable.

### 4. L-Theanine — Best for Racing Mind Sleepers

**What it is:** An amino acid found naturally in tea leaves (especially green tea). Unlike caffeine, it promotes relaxation without sedation.

**The evidence:** L-Theanine has solid evidence for reducing anxiety and improving focus during the day. For sleep specifically, a 2018 study in *Nutrients* found that L-Theanine supplementation improved sleep quality in children with ADHD. The evidence for healthy adults is more preliminary but promising.

**How it works:** L-Theanine increases alpha brain wave activity (associated with relaxed alertness) and boosts GABA, serotonin, and dopamine levels.

**Dose:** 100–400mg before bed. It's often stacked with magnesium for a "calm mind + relaxed body" combination.

## What Doesn't Work (Or Has Weak Evidence)

**Passionflower** — Limited human trials. Traditionally used for anxiety. Some small studies show mild benefit for sleep quality, but the evidence base is thin.

**GABA supplements** — Oral GABA doesn't cross the blood-brain barrier meaningfully. Any effect is likely indirect (via gut-brain signalling) or placebo.

**5-HTP** — Precursor to serotonin, which becomes melatonin. Indirect pathway. There's better evidence for melatonin itself.

**Kava** — Has some evidence for anxiety-related insomnia, but carries a risk of liver toxicity. Not recommended for regular use.

| Melatonin | ✅ Safe for short-term use (months) | Low |

**General rule:** Supplements aren't a substitute for sleep hygiene. If you need a supplement every night to sleep, that's worth discussing with your GP.

## Sleep Hygiene: The Supplement You Don't Have to Buy

No supplement will fully compensate for:

- **Inconsistent sleep schedules** (going to bed at different times)
- **Screen exposure before bed** (blue light suppresses melatonin)
- **Caffeine after 2pm** (caffeine has a half-life of 5–6 hours)
- **Alcohol before bed** (disrupts REM sleep, causes middle-of-the-night waking)

Try the basics first. Then add supplements on top.
